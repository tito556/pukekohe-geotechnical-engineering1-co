Raft and Mat Foundation Engineering for Pukekohe’s Volcanic Soils

Together, we solve the challenges of tomorrow.

DISCOVER →

The most expensive mistake we see in Pukekohe is a strip footing specified where a raft slab was obviously needed. The southern edge of town sits on deep Patumahoe clay loams that shrink and swell with the seasons, while pockets of soft alluvium along the Whangapouri Stream barely hold 50 kPa bearing. A standard perimeter footing in those conditions will differential-settle within the first two wet winters — we have remediated enough cracked slabs on Helvetia Road to know. Raft foundation design spreads the structural load across the entire building footprint, bridging local soft spots and reducing total and differential settlement to tolerances the superstructure can handle. We pair the raft geometry with a site-specific ground model built from borehole data, laboratory consolidation curves, and liquefaction screening per NZGS Module 1. This is not a one-size-fits-all slab: rib depth, edge thickening, and reinforcement layout respond directly to the soil profile and seismic demand defined in NZS 1170.5. For sites with marginal bearing, we also evaluate ground improvement through stone columns as a cost-effective alternative to deep piling before finalizing the raft thickness.

A raft foundation designed without site-specific subgrade reaction values is just a concrete slab guessing at the soil beneath it.

Process overview

Pukekohe’s microclimate matters more than most designers assume. Annual rainfall exceeds 1,200 mm, and the winter water table rises to within 1.0–1.5 m of ground surface across much of the low-lying residential belt. That sustained moisture feeds the expansive behaviour of the weathered basalt clays, which we typically classify as CH in the USCS system with plasticity indices above 25%. A raft foundation here must be designed as a stiffened slab with perimeter beams deep enough to remain below the active moisture zone — usually 600–900 mm in this district. We model the soil-structure interaction using modulus of subgrade reaction values derived from in-situ plate load tests or back-calculated from CPT tip resistance, rather than generic textbook tables. This matters because the volcanic residuum can vary from stiff clay to loose scoria within a single house lot. Our team integrates test pits early in the investigation to map the depth to basalt floaters, which can punch through an undersized raft if left undetected. We also specify a well-graded capillary break and sub-slab drainage to manage vapour and seasonal moisture cycling under the slab.

Local context

Pukekohe’s growth has pushed subdivisions onto former market-garden land where decades of cultivation have disturbed the upper 400–600 mm of soil. That reworked layer, combined with unmarked service trenches and old farm drains, creates a risk of differential settlement that a conventional footing cannot manage. The 2021 Pukekohe-Paerata Structure Plan accelerated development into areas where the Puketoka Formation and underlying Tauranga Group sediments inter-finger unpredictably, producing abrupt lateral changes in stiffness. A rigid raft foundation acts as a settlement-tolerant raft that bridges these transitions, but the design must account for angular distortion limits in the superstructure — we typically check against a maximum deflection ratio of 1/350 for masonry-clad buildings. Liquefaction-induced bearing loss is another driver for raft adoption: the NZGS-MBIE Module 4 framework often triggers a raft solution where SPT N-values drop below 10 in the upper 6 m, because a stiffened raft can tolerate some loss of support without structural failure, unlike isolated pad footings.

Top questions

How much does raft foundation design for a single residential lot in Pukekohe cost?

For a standard residential raft on a single lot in Pukekohe, a full geotechnical investigation and design report typically falls between NZ$1,950 and NZ$7,470. The spread depends on site access, the number of boreholes or CPT soundings required, and whether liquefaction triggering analysis is needed. Complex sites with basalt floaters or soft alluvium tend toward the upper end because we run additional lab testing and finite-element settlement modelling.

When does a raft foundation make more sense than deep piles in Pukekohe soils?

A raft becomes the better option when the depth to competent bearing exceeds about 3–4 metres and the structural loads are moderate — typical of residential and low-rise commercial buildings. In Pukekohe’s volcanic terrain, piles often hit basalt refusal at unpredictable depths, which drives up cost and schedule risk. A stiffened raft can bridge softer zones and floaters without penetrating the basalt, and we can combine it with a granular working platform to improve the shallow bearing response. Piles remain preferred for very high column loads or where liquefaction-induced settlement exceeds raft tolerances.

What site investigation data do you need to design a raft foundation properly?

We need at least two boreholes or a combination of boreholes and CPT soundings to a depth of 1.5 to 2 times the raft width, typically 6–15 m in Pukekohe. Laboratory testing must include one-dimensional consolidation on undisturbed samples, Atterberg limits, particle size distribution, and moisture content profiles. For seismic design we run SPT-based liquefaction screening per NZGS Module 1, and we often add hand-augered test pits to map the depth to basalt floaters in the upper 2 m. Without this level of investigation, the subgrade modulus and settlement predictions are guesswork.
